\begin{abstract}
Gradient-based optimization of satellite constellations has been considered intractable: coverage and revisit objectives are inherently discrete, and prior attempts using finite-difference gradients through black-box simulators found the problem ``extremely poorly conditioned'' for gradient methods.
We show that this intractability is an artifact of the non-differentiable objective formulation, not the underlying physics.
By introducing four continuous relaxations---a soft sigmoid visibility model, a noisy-OR multi-satellite aggregation, a leaky integrator for revisit gap tracking, and a LogSumExp soft-maximum---and composing them with the differentiable dSGP4 orbit propagator, we obtain an end-to-end differentiable pipeline from orbital elements to coverage metrics.
Unlike parametric constellation families (e.g.\ Walker-delta), this formulation naturally supports non-uniform, mission-specific objectives such as minimizing revisit time over weighted discrete target sets.
On a 12-satellite constellation optimized for priority-weighted targets, Adam reduces the worst-case revisit gap by 70\% in 300 iterations---orders of magnitude fewer evaluations than the simulated annealing and genetic algorithm baselines that previously dominated this problem class.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} satellite constellation design, differentiable programming, orbit propagation, coverage optimization, revisit time
