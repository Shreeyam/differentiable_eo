\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\subsection{Constellation Design and Optimization}

The constellation design problem has a long history.
\citet{walker1984satellite} introduced the Walker-delta pattern, parameterizing symmetric constellations by total satellites, number of planes, and a phasing parameter; this remains the most widely used family for uniform-coverage constellations.
\citet{mortari2004flower} generalized this with Flower constellations, which exploit compatible orbits to produce repeating ground tracks.
These parametric approaches are efficient but inherently symmetric: they cannot be tailored to non-uniform, mission-specific coverage requirements.

For general (non-parametric) constellation design, the literature has primarily relied on metaheuristic methods.
\citet{paek2019recon} optimized reconfigurable constellations (ReCon) for both global coverage and regional target revisit using simulated annealing (SA) and genetic algorithms (GA), formulating a multi-objective problem over five design variables including altitude, number of planes, satellites per plane, and field of regard.
Their SA required $\sim$250 iterations and GA required $\sim$50 generations with population size 50 ($\sim$2500 total evaluations), each involving expensive STK orbit simulations.
Critically, they also attempted gradient-based optimization using finite-difference approximations and found it ``extremely poorly conditioned,'' with convergence rarely achieved even from good initial points.
Other metaheuristic approaches include \citet{howell2006satellite} and \citet{lee2020satellite}; \citet{lang2024constellation} provide a comprehensive review and identify computational cost as the primary bottleneck.

Our work differs in two key respects.
First, we use \emph{true analytical gradients} through a differentiable propagator rather than finite-difference approximations through a black-box simulator, eliminating the poor conditioning that plagued prior gradient-based attempts.
Second, we make the coverage and revisit objectives themselves differentiable via continuous relaxations, rather than treating them as discrete metrics evaluated post-propagation.

\subsection{Differentiable Physics and Simulation}

Differentiable programming~\citep{baydin2018automatic} has transformed optimization in domains where the forward model is complex but smooth.
\citet{hu2020difftaichi} demonstrated differentiable physical simulators for robotics and fluid dynamics, and \citet{degrave2019differentiable} applied differentiable physics engines to contact-rich manipulation.
In the continuous-relaxation space, the Gumbel-Softmax trick~\citep{jang2017categorical} showed how to make discrete categorical choices differentiable, enabling gradient-based optimization of architectures and discrete structures.

In astrodynamics, \citet{acciarini2024dsgp4} introduced dSGP4, a PyTorch reimplementation of the SGP4/SDP4 orbit propagator in which every operation uses differentiable tensors.
They demonstrated gradient computation for state transition matrices and TLE fitting via Newton's method, but did not apply the framework to constellation-level coverage optimization.
Our work builds on dSGP4 by introducing the missing piece: differentiable coverage and revisit objectives that close the loop from orbital elements to mission performance metrics.
