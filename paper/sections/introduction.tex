\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Satellite constellation design---selecting orbital parameters for a fleet of satellites to optimize Earth observation performance---is a central problem in mission planning~\citep{wertz2011space}.
For missions requiring responsive coverage of specific regions or targets, the design challenge is acute: the constellation must provide frequent revisit over non-uniform, mission-specific areas of interest while respecting orbital mechanics constraints.
Applications range from disaster monitoring~\citep{paek2019recon} and maritime domain awareness to agricultural surveillance and defense reconnaissance.

The design space is high-dimensional.
For $N$ satellites with fixed altitude and eccentricity, the free parameters include each satellite's inclination, right ascension of the ascending node (RAAN), and mean anomaly---yielding $3N$ continuous decision variables.
Traditional approaches address this complexity in one of two ways.
Parametric families such as Walker-delta~\citep{walker1984satellite} and Flower constellations~\citep{mortari2004flower} reduce the design space to a handful of parameters but are inherently symmetric, making them unsuitable for non-uniform coverage objectives.
Metaheuristic methods---simulated annealing, genetic algorithms---can handle arbitrary objectives but require thousands to millions of forward evaluations through expensive orbit simulators~\citep{lang2024constellation, paek2019recon}.

Gradient-based optimization would seem a natural alternative, but it has been considered intractable for this problem.
\citet{paek2019recon} explicitly attempted gradient-based optimization of reconfigurable satellite constellations and found the problem ``extremely poorly conditioned for gradient-based methods,'' noting that ``even the solution convergence itself was rarely achieved.''
Their gradients were computed via finite differences through a black-box orbit propagation and coverage simulation, with coverage and revisit metrics defined as discrete, non-differentiable functions of the orbital parameters.

We argue that this intractability is an artifact of the \emph{objective formulation}, not the underlying physics.
The orbit propagation itself---specifically, the SGP4 model---is a composition of smooth trigonometric and polynomial functions with well-defined derivatives.
The recent dSGP4 library~\citep{acciarini2024dsgp4} makes these derivatives available via PyTorch automatic differentiation.
What remains non-differentiable are the coverage and revisit \emph{metrics}: the binary visibility indicator, the Boolean OR over satellites, the revisit gap computation, and the worst-case maximum.

We make three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Four continuous relaxations} that make coverage and revisit objectives differentiable: a soft sigmoid for binary visibility, a noisy-OR for multi-satellite aggregation, a leaky integrator for revisit gap tracking, and a LogSumExp soft-maximum for worst-case revisit (\figref{fig:relaxations}).
    \item \textbf{An end-to-end differentiable pipeline} from orbital elements to mission-specific coverage metrics, composed with the dSGP4 propagator, enabling gradient-based constellation optimization with standard tools (PyTorch autograd, Adam) (\figref{fig:pipeline}).
    \item \textbf{Demonstration on weighted target optimization}, a problem class where parametric constellation families are fundamentally inapplicable and where prior gradient-based attempts failed.  We show that first-order optimization succeeds when true analytical gradients are available through relaxed objectives, converging in 300 iterations versus thousands of evaluations for metaheuristic methods.
\end{enumerate}
