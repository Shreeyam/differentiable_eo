\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In conclusion, we show that gradient-based methods when composed with the appropriate relaxations and propagation techniques, are viable for design of satellite constellations.

The relaxed metrics introduced in this work track hard metrics (\secref{sec:relaxation_quality}) and produce well-conditioned gradients, in contrast to previous attempts using finite-difference approaches~\citep{paek_optimization_2019}.

On uniform coverage objectives with plane-accumulation constraints, we can derive Walker-Delta constellations from purely our differentiable objectives.

On weighted regional targets, the optimizer can discover asymmetric geometries, such as eccentric orbits that dwell over target latitudes, without requiring any hand-crafted inductive biases.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The current approach optimizes a single-epoch snapshot and does not account for long-term perturbations (J2 RAAN drift, atmospheric drag) that alter constellation geometry over longer time horizons. While excluded in this work by zeroing out relevant gradients, these factors can be included with appropriate metrics to evaluate correctness, for example, total spacecraft lifetime above a certain altitude in the case of atmospheric drag.
In this work, we discretize time steps, which introduces approximation error in the revisit metrics, requiring significant propagation over a fixed time horizon to acquire gradients. This can introduce noise into the gradients and require more compute, although still likely much less than for a different optimization method, like an evolutionary algorithm.
