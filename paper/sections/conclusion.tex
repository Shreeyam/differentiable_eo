\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We show that gradient-based optimization is viable for satellite constellation design when coverage and revisit metrics are made differentiable through continuous relaxations.
The relaxed objectives track their hard counterparts (\secref{sec:tightness}), produce well-conditioned gradients where finite-difference approaches do not~\citep{paek_optimization_2019}, and recover Walker Delta geometry on uniform coverage while discovering Molniya-like orbits for regional targets without domain-specific inductive biases.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The current approach optimizes a single-epoch snapshot and does not account for long-term perturbations (J2 RAAN drift, atmospheric drag) that alter constellation geometry over the mission lifetime.
Additionally, discrete design variables such as the number of orbital planes and satellites per plane remain non-differentiable and must be set by the user or searched over separately.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Incorporating J2 secular perturbations would enable optimization over longer time horizons, potentially discovering geometries that exploit RAAN drift for improved coverage evolution.
The differentiable pipeline presented is not specific to the objectives considered in this work: heterogeneous sensor placement, constellation reconfiguration, and multi-objective ground coverage with varying elevation constraints are all expressible as weighted sums over the same computational graph.
