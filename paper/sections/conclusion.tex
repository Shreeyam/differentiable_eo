\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Gradient-based optimization of satellite constellations has been considered intractable due to the discrete, non-differentiable nature of coverage and revisit objectives.
We showed that this intractability is an artifact of the objective formulation: by introducing four continuous relaxations and composing them with the differentiable $\partial$SGP4 orbit propagator, the entire pipeline becomes amenable to first-order optimization.
The relaxed surrogate closely tracks the discrete objective (\secref{sec:relaxation_quality}) and produces well-conditioned gradients where finite-difference approaches through discrete objectives~\citep{paek2019recon} do not.

On uniform coverage, the optimizer recovers Walker-optimal geometry from arbitrary initialization.
On weighted regional targets, it discovers asymmetric constellations---including eccentric orbits that dwell over target latitudes---that significantly outperform Walker baselines, without any domain-specific heuristics.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The current approach optimizes a single-epoch snapshot and does not account for long-term perturbations (J2 RAAN drift, atmospheric drag) that alter constellation geometry over months.
The time step discretization introduces approximation error in the revisit metrics.
While the loss landscape appears smooth near our solutions, we cannot rule out the existence of better local minima elsewhere in the parameter space.
The discrete design variables (number of planes, satellites per plane) remain non-differentiable and must be set by the user or searched over separately.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Incorporating J2 secular perturbations would enable optimization of long-term constellation maintenance, potentially discovering geometries that exploit RAAN drift for improved coverage evolution.
Multi-objective Pareto optimization would allow explicit trade-offs between competing mission requirements.
More broadly, the framework extends to constellation augmentation---optimizing new satellites to complement an existing fleet---a setting that completely breaks the symmetry assumptions of parametric families.
