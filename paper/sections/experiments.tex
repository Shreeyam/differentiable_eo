\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We first calibrate the relaxation hyperparameters to ensure the surrogate loss faithfully ranks candidate solutions. We then evaluate the framework on three experiments: a toy problem validating gradient correctness, a uniform-coverage baseline that recovers known-optimal geometries, and a weighted target optimization on a problem class where parametric families fail.

\subsection{Common Setup}
\label{sec:common_setup}

All experiments use dSGP4~\citep{acciarini2024dsgp4} for orbit propagation with PyTorch autograd for gradient computation.
Circular orbits are assumed ($e = 0.001$) at \SI{550}{\kilo\metre} altitude, held fixed by zeroing the mean-motion gradient.
The minimum elevation angle is $\alpha_{\min} = \SI{10}{\degree}$.
As described in \secref{sec:relaxation_quality}, we use separate sigmoid temperatures for coverage and revisit: the coverage temperature $\tau_{\text{cov}}$ controls gradient smoothness, while a tighter revisit temperature $\tau_{\text{rev}}$ prevents the leaky integrator from being gamed by phantom coverage.
The LogSumExp temperature is $\beta$.
Coverage and revisit metrics are evaluated over a \SI{24}{\hour} propagation horizon discretized into $K = 240$ time steps on a ground grid of $36 \times 72 = 2592$ points spanning $\pm\SI{70}{\degree}$ latitude.
Specific relaxation parameters, optimizer settings, and revisit weight $\lambda$ vary per experiment and are stated in each subsection.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}
\label{sec:relaxation_quality}

The composed relaxation has four hyperparameters that define the loss surface geometry: the coverage sigmoid temperature $\tau_{\text{cov}}$, the revisit sigmoid temperature $\tau_{\text{rev}}$ (introduced in \secref{sec:soft_visibility}), the LogSumExp temperature $\beta$, and the revisit weight $\lambda$.
We exclude optimizer dynamics (learning rate, momentum) from this calibration, as they affect convergence speed but not the shape of the loss landscape.
We calibrate by running four optimizations of a 24-satellite constellation ($6 \times 4$, inclination fixed at \SI{60}{\degree}, RAAN and mean anomaly free, per-plane RAAN sharing) from different initial RAAN configurations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Near-uniform}: $\Omega = [0, 60, 120, 180, 240, 300]$\SI{}{\degree} (close to Walker spacing),
    \item \textbf{Moderate}: $\Omega = [0, 30, 120, 200, 210, 300]$\SI{}{\degree} (irregular but spread),
    \item \textbf{Clustered}: $\Omega = [0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50]$\SI{}{\degree} (all planes within \SI{50}{\degree}),
    \item \textbf{Two-cluster}: $\Omega = [0, 5, 180, 185, 270, 275]$\SI{}{\degree} (three tight clusters).
\end{enumerate}
All runs use the same random mean anomaly initialization (seed 42) and default relaxation parameters ($\tau = \SI{2}{\degree}$, $\beta = \SI{10}{\minute}$, $\lambda = 0.1$) for 800 Adam iterations.

\paragraph{Ranking failure with shared softness.}
The near-uniform and moderate initializations converge to solutions with hard mean revisit gaps of $\sim$\SI{48}{\minute}, while the clustered and two-cluster initializations find solutions with gaps of $\sim$\SI{68}{\minute}.
However, under the default relaxation parameters, the \emph{relaxed} loss of the \SI{68}{\minute} solutions is \emph{lower} than that of the \SI{48}{\minute} solutions---the relaxation ranks the worse solutions as better.

This ranking failure is caused by the shared sigmoid temperature: the same $\tau$ controls both the coverage objective and the revisit leaky integrator (\eqref{eq:leaky_integrator}).
A soft sigmoid with $\tau = \SI{2}{\degree}$ assigns coverage $c_{ij} \approx 0.27$ to a satellite at $\alpha = \alpha_{\min} - \SI{2}{\degree}$.
For the coverage fraction (\eqref{eq:coverage_fraction}), this phantom coverage has minimal effect because it is a small additive bias.
But in the leaky integrator, it \emph{multiplicatively} resets the gap: $\Delta_j \leftarrow \Delta_j \cdot (1 - C_j)$.
When multiple clustered satellites each contribute partial coverage at low elevation, the noisy-OR combines them into substantial phantom coverage ($C_j \gg 0$) that partially resets the gap timer at every time step.
The clustered solutions benefit disproportionately from this effect.

\paragraph{Split-softness resolution.}
We decouple the sigmoid temperature into $\tau_{\text{cov}}$ for the coverage objective and $\tau_{\text{rev}}$ for the revisit integrator, then grid-search over $\tau_{\text{cov}} \in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0, 4.0, 5.0\}$\SI{}{\degree}, $\tau_{\text{rev}} \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$\SI{}{\degree}, $\beta \in \{3, 5, 7.5, 10, 15, 20\}$\SI{}{\minute}, and $\lambda \in \{0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ (1764 combinations, each requiring only four gradient-free forward passes).
A combination is \emph{valid} if it correctly ranks the good solutions (near-uniform and moderate) below the bad solutions (clustered and two-cluster).

\tabref{tab:hyperparam_tuning} summarizes the findings.
No valid combinations exist when $\tau_{\text{cov}} = \tau_{\text{rev}} \geq \SI{2}{\degree}$ (the original shared-softness regime).
Decoupling the temperatures resolves the ranking failure: combinations with $\tau_{\text{cov}} \geq \SI{3}{\degree}$ and $\tau_{\text{rev}} \leq \SI{0.5}{\degree}$ correctly rank solutions while maintaining smooth coverage gradients.
See Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparam_grid} for the full grid search results.

\paragraph{Smoothness--correctness tradeoff.}
The valid hyperparameter combinations tend toward tight revisit sigmoids ($\tau_{\text{rev}} \leq \SI{1}{\degree}$), which produce correct global rankings but sharp, poorly conditioned gradients.
In practice, optimization converges more reliably with softer values (e.g., $\tau_{\text{cov}} = \SI{5}{\degree}$, $\tau_{\text{rev}} = \SI{2}{\degree}$) that lie outside the valid regime.
This is not contradictory: the calibration tests \emph{global} ranking correctness across qualitatively different solutions, but gradient-based optimization only requires \emph{local} gradient quality within a basin of attraction.
Softer relaxations smooth the loss surface, improving convergence, while the underlying basin structure still guides the optimizer toward good solutions.
The hyperparameters used in subsequent experiments reflect this tradeoff, favoring reliable convergence over exact global ranking.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Toy Problem}
\label{sec:toy}

To validate that the relaxed objectives produce correct gradients and results, we consider the simplest non-trivial constellation design problem: two satellites in a single orbital plane at \SI{550}{\kilo\metre} altitude with inclination fixed at \SI{60}{\degree} and RAAN fixed at \SI{0}{\degree}, optimizing only their mean anomalies $\mathcal{M}_1$ and $\mathcal{M}_2$.
We use relaxation parameters $\tau_{\text{cov}} = \SI{5}{\degree}$, $\tau_{\text{rev}} = \SI{2}{\degree}$, $\beta = \SI{10}{\minute}$, $\lambda = 2.0$, and AdamW with learning rate $\eta = 10^{-2}$.

The optimal solution is known analytically: the two satellites should be separated by \SI{180}{\degree} in mean anomaly to maximize coverage and revisit rate within the shared orbital plane.
This reduces the problem to two free parameters, allowing the full loss landscape to be visualized as a 2D contour plot.

\paragraph{Loss landscape.}
We evaluate both the relaxed loss and hard (discrete) metrics on a dense grid over $(\mathcal{M}_1, \mathcal{M}_2) \in [0, 360)^2$\SI{}{\degree}.
\figref{fig:toy_landscape} shows the resulting landscapes: the top row displays the relaxed loss, coverage, and revisit, while the bottom row shows the corresponding hard metrics.
Both landscapes exhibit a clear global minimum along the anti-diagonal $|\mathcal{M}_1 - \mathcal{M}_2| = \SI{180}{\degree}$, with no spurious local minima.
The relaxed landscape is visibly smoother than the hard landscape while preserving the same basin structure, confirming that the relaxations provide a smooth surface amenable to gradient descent without distorting the underlying objective.

\paragraph{Gradient descent trajectory.}
Starting from $\mathcal{M}_1 = \SI{120}{\degree}$, $\mathcal{M}_2 = \SI{130}{\degree}$ (a \SI{10}{\degree} separation), Adam converges to $|\mathcal{M}_1 - \mathcal{M}_2| \approx \SI{180}{\degree}$ within 400 iterations.
The trajectory (overlaid in white on \figref{fig:toy_landscape}) follows the gradient field smoothly toward the minimum without oscillation.
This validates that the composed relaxations produce well-behaved gradients---a prerequisite for the higher-dimensional experiments that follow.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Uniform Coverage Baseline}
\label{sec:uniform}

Before applying the method to non-uniform objectives, we establish a baseline on the well-understood uniform coverage problem.
This validates that the optimizer can recover known-optimal geometries and provides a point of comparison for the weighted-target experiment.

We optimize $N = 24$ satellites in 6 orbital planes (4 per plane) with inclination fixed at \SI{60}{\degree}.
RAAN and mean anomaly are free, with RAAN shared within each plane, yielding $6 + 24 = 30$ effective degrees of freedom.
We use AdamW with learning rate $\eta = 10^{-2}$ and revisit weight $\lambda = 0.1$.

\paragraph{Initial configuration.}
We deliberately initialize the constellation with irregular RAAN offsets $[0, 30, 120, 200, 210, 300]$\SI{}{\degree} (far from uniform \SI{60}{\degree} spacing) and random mean anomalies (seed 42).
The classical Walker-delta $24/6/1$ pattern uses \SI{60}{\degree} RAAN spacing.

\paragraph{Results.}
Over 1000 iterations, the optimizer recovers near-uniform RAAN spacing from the irregular initialization, approaching the Walker-delta geometry without domain-specific heuristics.
\tabref{tab:uniform_results} summarizes the metrics; \figref{fig:convergence} shows convergence, spatial coverage redistribution, and ground tracks.

\paragraph{Loss landscape.}
Following \citet{li2018visualizing}, we visualize the loss landscape by computing per-trajectory principal components and evaluating the landscape on each trajectory's own 2D slice through the 30-dimensional parameter space.
Unlike a single global PCA projection---which discards the dimensions that matter for individual trajectories---each slice faithfully represents the landscape that its optimizer actually navigated.
\figref{fig:landscape} shows smooth, well-conditioned basins across all four initializations, in contrast to the ``extremely poorly conditioned'' landscape reported by \citet{paek2019recon} when using finite-difference gradients through discrete objectives.
This confirms that the poor conditioning observed in prior work was a consequence of the non-differentiable objective formulation, not the underlying physics.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Regional Target Optimization}
\label{sec:weighted}

The primary advantage of gradient-based constellation optimization over parametric families is the ability to handle non-uniform, mission-specific objectives.
We demonstrate this by optimizing a small constellation for coverage and revisit over Europe---a problem that Walker constellations cannot address by construction.

\paragraph{Setup.}
We sample 500 ground targets from European country boundaries (GeoJSON), weighted by $\cos(\text{lat})$ to correct for area distortion.
The constellation consists of 4 satellites in 2 orbital planes (2 per plane).
Unlike Experiment~2, we free all orbital geometry parameters: inclination ($\iota \in [30, 90]$\SI{}{\degree}), RAAN, mean anomaly, argument of perigee, and orbital shape.
To allow the optimizer to explore eccentric orbits without producing unphysical perigee altitudes, we reparameterize eccentricity and altitude jointly as (perigee altitude, excess altitude), where perigee $\in [400, 600]$\SI{}{\kilo\metre} and excess $\in [0, 1500]$\SI{}{\kilo\metre}, guaranteeing perigee $\geq$ \SI{400}{\kilo\metre} by construction.
Inclination, RAAN, eccentricity, argument of perigee, and altitude are shared within each plane; mean anomaly is free per satellite, giving 12 effective degrees of freedom.
We use AdamW with $\eta = 10^{-2}$, $\lambda = 1.0$, and run for 2000 iterations.

\paragraph{Results.}
Starting from a Walker-like circular initialization at \SI{550}{\kilo\metre}, the optimizer discovers a qualitatively different geometry (\tabref{tab:weighted_results}).
Both planes converge to eccentric orbits ($e \approx 0.09$) with argument of perigee $\omega \approx \SI{270}{\degree}$, placing apogee over the northern hemisphere where the satellite dwells longest.
The resulting orbits have perigee $\approx$ \SI{580}{\kilo\metre} and apogee $\approx$ \SI{1960}{\kilo\metre}---a Molniya-like dwell strategy discovered entirely from gradients, with no domain-specific heuristics.
The inclination converges to $\sim$\SI{64}{\degree}, matching European latitudes.

The optimized constellation achieves 38.2\% coverage and 41.9~min mean revisit over Europe, compared to 10.0\% coverage and 247.5~min for a Walker 4/2/1 baseline---a $4\times$ improvement in coverage and $6\times$ in revisit with the same number of satellites.

\figref{fig:europe_density} shows the visibility density (number of timesteps each ground bin is within \SI{10}{\degree} elevation of any satellite).
The optimized constellation concentrates coverage in the northern hemisphere over Europe, while the Walker baseline distributes coverage symmetrically.
The density map also reveals longitudinal structure: the orbital period of $\sim$\SI{111}{\minute} produces approximately 13 revolutions per sidereal day, creating a near-resonant ground track that clusters revisits at preferred longitudes.
